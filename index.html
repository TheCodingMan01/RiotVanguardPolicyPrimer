Fair Play or Full Access?

Every League of Legends player has clicked “Accept” to install Riot Vanguard, a security tool that digs deep into your computer. But few realise just how deep that goes.

As players, we love fair competition. No one wants to lose to a cheater, its straight up not enjoyable. But the anti cheat software that promises fairness can also become one of the most intrusive programs on your PC. Vanguard runs below your antivirus, with more power than most operating system tools. Once you agree, Riot gains permission to scan and evaluate what’s happening inside your machine constantly.

Law professor Lawrence Lessig once said that “code is law.” What he meant is that technology’s design can decide what we can or can’t do. Riot Vanguard works exactly like that. It enforces rules not by trust or discussion, but through architecture either you accept the terms, or you don’t play at all.

What is Riot Vanguard?

Think of Vanguard as a referee that never leaves your PC. It’s Riot’s anti cheat software that launches every time you turn on your computer. It monitors what’s running, what hardware is attached, and what programs might be interfering with the game.

Example

Players on Reddit have shared stories of Vanguard blocking harmless software like fan controllers or keyboard drivers, forcing them to uninstall other tools just to play. It’s doing its job but a little too well.

So what?

According to Riot, this deep access keeps the game fair. Cheats often hide at this same low level, so the anti cheat must meet them there. But it also means a company has the same power over your device that your operating system does. Lessig called this an “architecture of control”: behaviour is shaped not by policy but by what the software allows.

The Mozilla Foundation’s Privacy Not Included report (2023) calls this kind of design “quiet surveillance.” It’s not visible, but it’s always on and it’s becoming normalised as the price of convenience or safety.

What you accept when you click “I Agree”

Most of us scroll past Terms of Service pages, but Vanguard’s fine print deserves attention. Installing it means giving permission for persistent monitoring of your system, even when the game is closed. Riot’s privacy notice allows collection of data like your running applications, hardware setup, and even behaviour patterns that suggest cheating.

Example

You might not realise that Vanguard loads before you even log in to Windows. It starts with your PC and doesn’t fully stop until you uninstall both the driver and the game. There’s no partial opt out.

So what?

This IS NOT real consent. It’s a condition. An ultimatum if you will. If you want to play, you have to agree. Privacy researcher Tjerk Dobber (2023) calls this “algorithmic efficiency” when systems take away choice under the excuse of protecting users. Stardust, Gillett and Albury (2023) describe something similar on dating apps: surveillance presented as safety. Vanguard applies that same logic to gaming.

Why it matters

Privacy isn’t about hiding something bad. It’s about keeping control of your digital life. When kernel level surveillance becomes normal, we stop noticing how much power we’ve given away.

So what?

As media scholar János Bayer (2020) warns, hidden systems harm both those who see and those who don’t. In League of Legends, you don’t see what Vanguard records, how it decides someone’s cheating, or where that data goes. The system acts as judge, jury and enforcer all in code you can’t inspect.

Lessig’s “code is law” idea helps explain why that matters. Instead of governments setting limits, private architecture quietly defines them. Riot decides what’s fair; users just comply. This shifts accountability away from public oversight and toward opaque algorithms.

And it’s not only games. Schools and workplaces use similar monitoring tools to track students and employees. When we accept this in games, we make it easier to accept elsewhere. As Stardust and colleagues note, surveillance sold as safety can easily turn into surveillance as control.

What you can do

Fair play shouldn’t mean giving up your privacy. Here’s how you can push back:

  • Read the fine print: take five minutes to open Riot’s Privacy Notice and see exactly what’s being collected.
  • Limit exposure: avoid installing kernel level software on your main work or banking computer.
  • Ask for transparency: request that Riot publish reports showing what data Vanguard stores and for how long.
  • Support stronger regulation: back efforts to treat kernel anti cheat like other high risk technologies, such as facial recognition.
  • Do a data detox: Mozilla’s Data Detox Kit suggests reviewing which apps have the deepest access to your system and cutting that list down.

As Lessig so aptly put it, we can either let these architectures define our freedoms or demand technologies that respect user choice. The next time you click “Accept,” think about what you’re really agreeing to.

Fair play should NEVER require full access.
